Product case study

Saga: zero to product.

Saga is a continuous AI-moderated UX research platform: always-on five-minute interviews with real users, scored against a fixed rubric, delivered as plain-English issues and fixes. I built it solo, end to end: the research methodology, the product strategy, the pricing, the market sizing, the unit economics, and the working software.

01 · The problem and the thesis

Analytics tells you what happened. It never tells you why.

Product teams are drowning in UX debt: the friction, confusion, and loss of trust users experience every day that rarely shows up directly in a dashboard. The quantitative side is continuous and cheap. The qualitative side arrives late and episodic: a metric drops, tickets spike, someone finally commissions a study, and the findings land weeks after the damage.

The thesis: make qualitative signal continuous. Run always-on AI interviews with real users while the experience is fresh, score every conversation on a consistent rubric, and turn the stream into a durable, trendable measure of UX health.

The differentiator

Every other research tool is reactive: you program it with questions and it returns answers, so the output is only as good as the question the team thought to ask. Saga is proactive. The foundational question layer, the product's vital signs (Ease, Trust, Value), ships fixed, built from 11 years of research practice. Customers do not program it, and that is the point: fixed questions are what make scores trendable over time and benchmarkable across companies.

02 · The product

Five-minute interviews, a living grade, and fixes you can act on.

Always-on interviews

A widget intercepts users in-product while the experience is fresh. An AI moderator runs a five-to-seven minute conversation: no forms, no leading questions, max 14 turns. Question sequences are pre-built; the state machine probes, redirects, or wraps based on how the participant engages.

A consistent, trendable score

Every conversation is scored on Ease, Trust, and Value against fixed anchors. Re-scoring the same interview lands within roughly half a point (0.12 SD measured across hundreds of interviews), which is what makes the trend line trustworthy. Customers see a letter grade per dimension, A+ to F, weighted so one catastrophic interview meaningfully pulls it down.

Issues, not transcripts

Findings across interviews are clustered by root cause, not surface wording, and promoted to Issues by a severity-gated rule: one user saying "I did not buy because..." is a real lost sale and promotes immediately; an annoyance needs to repeat before it earns attention. Every issue ships with a recommended fix in plain English.

Ask the corpus

A natural-language Q&A surface over the redacted interview corpus: "why are users not coming back?" returns an answer with citations to real sessions, a confidence tier, and stakes. Hallucinated citations are filtered before the answer renders.

What it is not: surveys, analytics, A/B testing, or research-ops workflow. Saga sits in the gap those tools leave: the why behind the what.

03 · How it works

Ten agents, and a rule: nothing reaches a reader unverified.

Every interview runs through a chain of specialized agents: a moderator conducts the conversation, a redactor strips PII before anything else can read it, scoring rates the interview, synthesis extracts evidence, and a single writer agent (the Scribe) authors every customer-facing string in one consistent voice. Between every generation step and its consumer sits a gate.

Moderator → [gate: interview quality] → Redactor → Scoring → [gate: score verification] →
Synthesis → Scribe → [gate: fresh-context grounding + jargon scan] → [deterministic write-guard] → Dashboard
Fabrication defense. The grounding gate re-verifies the Scribe's output against the transcript in a fresh context: any quote that is not verbatim, any claim without support, gets rewritten or dropped. A final deterministic guard (schema + banned-pattern scan) sits in front of the database and is never skipped.
Privacy by construction. The redactor is a mandatory gate: a regex pass for deterministic patterns, then an LLM pass for contextual PII, validated at 100% recall on a planted-PII corpus. Audio never leaves the browser; only text is transmitted.
Spend where it shows. One model decision I am proud of: the moderator, the single participant-facing surface, runs on the premium model because an A/B showed clearly more natural phrasing. Everything else runs on cheaper models. The 5× premium lands on exactly one call per turn.
Methodology as code. The moderator, its inspector, and the fix-finding agent share a research-methodology module: evidence levels (what users say < feel < do), a ban on leading and probabilistic questions, behavior weighted over self-report. 22 unit tests pin the framework so a refactor cannot silently drop a rule.

The operating rule of the whole pipeline: a claim that cannot be grounded in a verbatim participant quote is not a finding.

04 · The pivot

I froze a feature-complete product. That was the right call.

Saga started as a Shopify app for SMB merchants at $49 to $99 a month: interview shoppers, find the friction costing sales. I built it feature-complete on a dev store. Then the customer-development evidence pointed somewhere else.

Pull signal

Founders and mid-market design leaders upmarket said "we would have paid for that" unprompted. SMB merchants were interested; tech companies without research teams had budget and urgency.

Economics

ACV ceiling of ~$1,200/yr on Shopify vs $6K to $70K+ upmarket, on the same cost base. The margin profile was simply better where the stakes were higher.

Platform risk

App Store distribution meant living inside someone else's review queue, ranking algorithm, and take rate. Direct SaaS distribution removed the dependency entirely.

In May 2026 I froze the Shopify build and re-aimed the product at design-mature tech companies with little to no in-house research function: teams that ship fast, care about craft, and have nobody covering the qualitative side. The agents, scoring, and pipeline carried over unchanged; the wrapper, pricing, and go-to-market were rebuilt. Sunk cost is not a strategy.

05 · Pricing

Three pricing models in one month, each one a lesson.

Pricing was the hardest product problem in the company, and I treated it like one: shipped, measured against willingness-to-pay logic, rewritten.

v1

Named persona tiers. Startup / Mid-Market / Upper Mid-Market with narrative labels. Problem: buyers stall on "am I really Mid-Market?" and the label invites negotiation.

v2

Flat self-serve grid. Removed the ambiguity, but flattened too far: a $50M-revenue company paid the same per-surface rate as a $5M startup, even though stakes and budget differed by 10×. It under-discriminated on willingness-to-pay.

v3

Revenue-gated persona pricing (shipped). The persona is set by a numeric gate, not a narrative: revenue when known, headcount as fallback, funding raised to catch pre-revenue companies with budget. Take the highest band any signal indicates. The bill is base fee + (surfaces × depth, on the persona's price book) + researcher hours + query overage.

The spread is the point. The same product configuration bills 1 : 2.7 : 5.9 across personas, driven entirely by a company attribute. Capability never differs by tier; only the rate does. Every tier gets every depth option and full agent access.

The gate polices itself. Self-serve stays honor-system: no revenue interrogation. Any real mid-market company will need a contract, a DPA, and SSO, and the moment they ask, they have self-sorted into the right tier.

Per-seat pricing, explicitly rejected. AI erodes seat pricing: agents query on behalf of the whole org, so seats stop mapping to value. Queries are pooled org-wide instead, human and agent alike.

The floor has a reason. $400/mo minimum, because below that, lifetime value does not clear payment processing, support, and onboarding. A price that loses money on every customer is not a growth strategy.

06 · Market

A market you can defend beats a market that impresses.

The honest version: "150 million startups globally" counts every LLC, and Fortune-500 design teams with mature in-house research are somebody else's market. Saga's market is English-active tech companies with a digital product, real users, and no research function.

Where the dollars are. The startup tier is the wedge: hot pull-signal, low CAC, fast learning. But upper mid-market (500 to 2,000-person tech companies with a design function) carries roughly 60% of the SAM at a ~$60K blended ACV. Volume comes from below; revenue comes from above.

Weak points labeled as weak. The sizing doc flags its own softest number: the upper mid-market population is an order-of-magnitude estimate pending primary-source validation, and it says so in bold, with the follow-up work named. Researcher's honesty applied to a founder's spreadsheet.

The real cost driver is human, not model. The included researcher hours (spot-check and trend interpretation, deliberately not per-interview review) dominate COGS at the entry tier. So researcher time is metered everywhere, and contracts include an annual repricing clause to pass through model-cost changes.

COGS is not the binding constraint; CAC is. At a $400 to $800 entry point, acquisition has to run on content, founder distribution, and product-led growth, not paid channels. The economics doc says this out loud instead of hiding it under a margin table.

08 · What I said no to

The product is defined by what it refuses to do.

Customer-authored questions: never.

Question design is the IP and the source of agent quality. It is also what keeps scores comparable across time and across companies. The day customers write the questions, the trend line dies.

A feature I built, then killed.

Brainstorm-a-Fix was a conversational fix-finding agent, fully built with a closed feedback loop. A design-partner customer told us founders already have their own AI with full company context; they wanted the evidence, not the brainstorm. Deprecated it. Building it taught us the loop; killing it kept the product honest.

Severity labels users never see.

Severity drives the promotion math internally, but customers never see "URGENT" pills. They see two things they can reason about: how many users hit this, and what it is estimated to cost. Alarm without evidence is how dashboards lose trust.

Audience filters, dropped on customer evidence.

Design-partner feedback showed slicing belongs in the customer's stack, not ours. They export the JSON and cut it their way; we spend the roadmap on the parts only we can do.
